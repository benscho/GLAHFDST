Lake Erie Walleye Habitat Suitability Index (HSI)



Tags
Great Lakes, Laurentian Great Lakes, framework, inlandWaters, planningCadastre, Erie, walleye, habitat suitability index, HSI


Summary 

The walleye habitat suitability index model, from Pandit et al. (2013), applied using data from the GLAHF database covering all of Lake Erie.  Note, in the Pandit et al. (2013) the model was only applied to the Ontario waters of the western and eastern basins of Lake Erie.


Description 

The walleye habitat suitability models built by Pandit et al. (2013) used walleye gillnet sampling data from the Ontario Ministry of Natural Resources (OMNR) and Ohio Department of Natural Resources (ODNR). The model was built from data collected in the Ontario waters of the western and eastern basins of Lake Erie and the manuscript's results were confined to these areas. These layers represent results using GLAHF data applied to the extents of the waters of Lake Erie using the linear regression interaction model reported in the paper for juvenile, adult, and all walleye. Several models were reported in the manuscript, but the best fit model was the linear regression interaction models.The linear regression interaction model uses the variables depth, secchi, dissolved oxgen, and temperature and applying surface and bottom dissolved oxygen and temperature variables for surface and bottom specific results. For secchi and dissolved oxygen (surface and bottom) interpolated rasters were calculated from Environment Canada water quality sampling data (https://www.ec.gc.ca/grandslacs-greatlakes/) averaged over the months of July and August for the years 2006-2010. The bathymetry layer was from the National Ocean and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) National Centers for Environmental Information (https://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/mgg/greatlakes/greatlakes.html). The surface temperature layer is the average of daily mean surface temperature for July-August 2006-2010 from the Great Lakes CoastWatch Great Lakes Surface Environmental Analysis (http://coastwatch.glerl.noaa.gov/glsea/glsea.html). The benthic temperature was calculated from the bottom layer of the vertical water temperature model from NOAA Great Lakes Coastal Forecasting System (http://www.glerl.noaa.gov/res/glcfs/) for the same timeframe. All data layers were standardized to the GLAHF spatial framework at a resolution of 30 m.
